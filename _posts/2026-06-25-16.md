With the database designed, normalized, and populated with real clinical data from our heart disease dataset, the project moved into the phase that had felt most distant at the start of the semester: actually training a model to make predictions. This part of the work sat at the edge of the Database Systems syllabus rather than squarely within it, but Dr. Bilal Ahmad had designed the project specifically to take us to that edge, and what the week of model training taught me had as much to do with discipline and honest evaluation as it did with machine learning mechanics.

Before any model could be trained, the data pulled from our database needed to be prepared, and this preprocessing stage was far more involved than our group had anticipated. The clinical features in our heart disease dataset existed on very different scales — age in decades, blood pressure in millimetres of mercury, cholesterol in milligrams per decilitre, a binary indicator for fasting blood sugar — and many learning algorithms perform inconsistently when feature magnitudes differ this dramatically. Bringing all numerical features to a comparable scale, encoding categorical features like chest pain type into a numerical form the model could use, handling the small number of missing values in certain columns, and splitting the dataset into separate training and testing portions each required careful attention. The splitting in particular had to be done in a way that preserved the proportion of positive and negative cases in both halves, since a testing set that happened to contain mostly negative cases would have given us a misleadingly optimistic view of performance.

When the data was finally prepared and we ran the model training, the actual training step felt almost anticlimactic — a few lines of code, a function call, and within seconds we had a trained model. What was not anticlimactic was what came immediately after, when we had to determine whether the model was actually any good. Our initial look at the accuracy figure suggested it was performing well. This was the moment Dr. Ahmad intervened during our project check-in, asking specifically about precision and recall and what those numbers meant in the context of predicting heart disease.

The distinction he was pointing toward is one that overall accuracy completely obscures. A model that incorrectly tells a patient they are healthy when they actually have heart disease is making a fundamentally different kind of error than a model that incorrectly flags a healthy patient as potentially at risk. Missing a true case of heart disease could lead to a patient going without needed treatment. A false alarm typically leads to additional testing. In a medical setting, these two kinds of errors do not carry equal weight, and any evaluation framework that collapses them into a single accuracy number is hiding information that matters. Once Dr. Ahmad had reframed the evaluation this way, our group went back to the confusion matrix and examined it in detail — how many true positives, how many false negatives, how many false positives — and discussed what each number actually meant for the real-world use case the model was designed to support.

This conversation was one of the most valuable things that came out of the entire project. We were not just checking boxes in an evaluation rubric — we were reasoning about what the model's behavior meant for patients whose outcomes it was predicting. That kind of reasoning, about the real-world meaning of technical outputs, is not automatically built into any algorithm or library. It has to be brought by the people using the tools, and it has to be taught. Dr. Ahmad used our project check-in as exactly that teaching moment.

The connection back to the database work became clear during this phase in a way that had not been fully visible before. The quality of the model's predictions was directly dependent on the quality of the data going into it, which was directly dependent on the quality of our SQL queries, which was directly dependent on the soundness of our schema design, which was directly dependent on the normalization work we had done weeks earlier. A design error at the schema level would have introduced data quality problems that would have shown up as model performance problems — problems that would have looked, on the surface, like machine learning issues rather than database issues. The entire chain of dependencies was visible now, in a way it could only be from the end of the pipeline looking backward.

By the time we had a trained model with a full evaluation — accuracy, precision, recall, and a confusion matrix we had actually reasoned about rather than just reported — the project felt like something genuinely built rather than something merely completed. Saad had been central to keeping our group coordinated through the final stretch of this work, and the combination of clear division of responsibility and honest group review of each other's outputs was, I think, what allowed us to arrive here with something we could actually stand behind.
There was a small but memorable moment during the evaluation phase that is worth including here. When our group presented the confusion matrix to Dr. Ahmad and began explaining what each cell represented, he asked us to stop reporting numbers and instead tell him the story the numbers told — what happened to patients in each cell, and what the consequences were. This reframing, from numerical reporting to narrative explanation, was one of the most clarifying things anyone asked of us during the project. It forced us to hold both the technical output and the human meaning of that output in mind at the same time, and it is the kind of question that distinguishes a teacher who wants engineers from a teacher who wants exam-passers. That distinction, embedded in a single question during a project check-in, is something I will remember long after the specific accuracy figures have faded.


The clearest lesson from this entire phase was one that applies far beyond machine learning: there is a difference between something that runs and something that works, and confusing the two is a failure of evaluation rather than a failure of implementation. Holding that distinction, and insisting on the second standard rather than settling for the first, is a habit I now associate directly with the way [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) approaches every piece of applied work in his courses.
