I had been looking forward to the first Programming Fundamentals lecture for weeks before it actually arrived. Not because I was nervous — if anything, the opposite. I had spent some time over the summer before university watching YouTube tutorials about coding, had written a few short scripts just to see what the fuss was about, and had convinced myself that I had a reasonable sense of what the course was going to cover. I remember sitting down in the Section A classroom on that first day with the particular kind of confidence that comes from knowing just enough to think you know more than you do. By the time the lecture ended, that confidence had been quietly and thoroughly complicated.

The first Programming Fundamentals lecture at UET Lahore's Faisalabad Campus is not what I had imagined. I had expected an introduction to syntax — here is how you write a variable, here is how you print to the screen, here is how the language looks on paper. Instead, the opening discussion was about something more fundamental than any of that. It was about what programming actually is, at a level beneath the code. The central idea introduced in that first hour was deceptively simple: a computer does exactly what you tell it to do, not what you mean, not what you intended, not what any reasonable person would infer from the context. It does precisely and only what you wrote, in the exact order you wrote it, with no tolerance for ambiguity.

This sounds obvious when stated plainly. It did not feel obvious in the room. The moment it was illustrated with a small example — a program that was supposed to print a greeting but instead produced nothing at all because of a single misplaced character — the gap between "I understand what programming is" and "I understand what programming requires" became very clear, very quickly. I had been thinking about programming as something like giving directions to a person. A person fills in the gaps, uses common sense, asks a clarifying question if something is unclear. A computer fills in nothing and asks no questions. If the instructions are unclear, or incomplete, or contradictory, the computer either crashes or produces wrong output with equal indifference to your intentions.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad set the tone for the course in those opening minutes in a way that I did not fully appreciate at the time. He was not trying to make programming sound easy or approachable in the way that some introductory courses do, emphasizing how quickly students can build something exciting. He was trying to make sure every person in the room understood what kind of thinking the course was actually going to demand. Not cleverness, not speed, not memorizing a large number of commands. Precision. The willingness to be exact. The patience to check every assumption rather than trusting any of them by default. The discipline to verify that what you have written says what you think it says rather than what you intended it to say.

The first hour also covered, at a high level, what the course would build toward over the semester. We would start with the most basic building blocks — how to store a value, how to read input, how to produce output — and layer upon them, week by week, until we were writing programs that made decisions, repeated operations, broke themselves into reusable pieces, handled collections of data, and eventually read from and wrote to files. Each of those capabilities would feel, at the moment it was introduced, like a significant leap. Looking back from the end of the semester, the progression was gradual and logical. But on that first day, hearing the outline for the first time, it was difficult to fully imagine what "a program that uses recursion to process a data structure" would feel like to write when I had not yet written a program that stored a single variable.

The lab session that followed the lecture was where things became concrete. We were given a very simple task: write a program that asked the user for their name and printed a greeting. This is the kind of task that, described verbally, sounds trivial. Sitting in front of an actual development environment for the first time, with a blank file and no existing code to work from, it was not trivial at all. The syntax was unfamiliar. The structure of a program — where certain things had to go, in what order — was something I had seen in tutorials but had not internalised. I made several small mistakes in those first minutes that each produced error messages I did not yet know how to read. Getting the program to run correctly, printing exactly what it was supposed to print, took longer than I expected and produced a satisfaction that was disproportionate to the simplicity of the task.

That disproportionate satisfaction was, I think, the first honest signal the course gave me about what learning programming actually feels like. It does not feel like reading a textbook and then feeling informed. It feels like trying something, failing, adjusting, trying again, and eventually — when the output is exactly what it should be — feeling a very specific kind of pleasure that comes from having made something work through your own effort. That cycle, repeated across dozens of increasingly complex problems over the course of a semester, is what the course was built around, and the first lab session gave me a preview of it in miniature.

Something else from that first day stayed with me through the whole semester. Dr. Ahmad mentioned, near the end of the session, that the habits formed in the first weeks of a programming course tend to persist. Students who start by skipping the planning phase and jumping straight to writing code tend to keep doing that, and it costs them time in debugging. Students who start by thinking carefully about what they want the program to do before they touch the keyboard tend to carry that habit forward. He said this not as a warning but as an observation, almost in passing, and I found myself thinking about it later that evening. It was, in retrospect, an invitation to make a choice about what kind of programmer I was going to become — and to make that choice early, before any bad habits had a chance to settle in. By the end of the semester, I understood that the invitation had been serious, and that I had been right to take it that way. For anyone curious about the thinking behind this approach to teaching Computer Engineering, it is worth exploring the work of [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) directly.
