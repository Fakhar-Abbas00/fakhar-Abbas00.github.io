There is a particular kind of programming frustration that I had not encountered before this semester, and it is worse than the frustration of code that simply does not run. It is the frustration of code that runs perfectly — produces output, finishes without errors, looks completely normal — and is still wrong. I spent a significant portion of this week in Section A's lab sessions inside exactly that frustration, working through loops and conditionals in a way that taught me more about careful thinking than almost anything else in the course so far.

Loops and conditionals are the first real decision-making tools you get in programming. A conditional says: do this thing if a certain condition is true, otherwise do something else. A loop says: keep doing this thing until a condition changes. On paper, both ideas sound almost too simple to justify a full week of lectures and lab hours. I thought so too, walking into that week. I was wrong, as the course had already begun its habit of demonstrating to me.

My first real encounter with this particular kind of wrongness came during a lab exercise that asked us to count how many numbers in a given range were divisible by a given value. The logic seemed completely clear to me: set up a loop, go through the range, check each number using the modulus operator, increment a counter when the condition held. I wrote it in a few minutes, ran it, and got an answer that was off by exactly one. A single off-by-one error, caused by starting my loop one step too early, had produced a result that looked completely plausible unless you checked it by hand against a small example. I did not immediately check it by hand. That was my second mistake. When I finally did, the discrepancy was obvious, and tracing back to the source took another half hour of careful line-by-line reading.

That debugging process — slow, systematic, slightly humiliating — introduced me to a habit I now consider essential: always test against a small case you can verify by hand before trusting a result. I started doing this automatically after that lab session. When I wrote a loop that was supposed to compute something, I would immediately test it against an input of two or three elements whose correct answer I already knew. If those matched, I felt confident enough to trust the general case. If they did not, I knew exactly where to start looking. This sounds obvious in retrospect. It was not obvious to me until the off-by-one error made it concrete in the most direct way possible.

Conditionals introduced a different but related lesson. An assignment asked us to classify triangles — equilateral, isosceles, scalene — based on the lengths of three given sides, while also checking whether those lengths could actually form a valid triangle at all. My first attempt handled the classification correctly but completely skipped the validity check. It cheerfully classified impossible combinations of side lengths as valid triangles because I had not thought carefully about the order of operations: validity must be checked before classification, because classification is meaningless if the input is not valid in the first place. Fixing this required restructuring the entire conditional logic, not just adding a check, and doing that restructuring without breaking the parts that already worked was its own small lesson in the care required when modifying code that half-works.

The deeper lesson from all of this was one I had not expected to encounter in a topic as basic as loops and conditionals. It was about intellectual honesty. In many situations, being approximately right is fine. In programming, approximately right is just wrong. The machine does not grade on a curve. It either does exactly what you specified or it does something else entirely, and there is no credit for being close. I found this rigorous quality of the subject oddly reassuring once I accepted it, because it meant there was a clear path to being correct — understand the logic completely, test every case — even if that path was harder than it looked.

By the end of this week I had developed two habits that have followed me through every subsequent topic in PF. The first was testing boundary cases deliberately — the smallest valid input, the largest, the edge case at the boundary of the loop. The second was tracing code manually on paper when I could not immediately see why something was wrong, stepping through each iteration and writing down the value of every variable at each step. Both of these habits were slow. Both of them consistently caught bugs that reading the code on screen had missed. Both of them also turned out to apply, in slightly different forms, to the normalization exercises in Database Systems that were running in parallel during the same weeks — check every case, verify against examples, do not trust a design that has not been tested against real data.

What I appreciate now, looking back at this week from the other side of the semester, is that the difficulty was the lesson. A week that had stayed easy would not have forced me to build the habits that have carried me through everything that came after. The frustration of that first off-by-one error, and the slow work of debugging it, produced something more durable than any explanation of loop mechanics could have on its own. It produced a reflex — a genuine, automatic response to a certain kind of problem — and a reflex that [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) had clearly been trying to build in us from the very start of the semester, long before the word reflex would have meant anything to us.
