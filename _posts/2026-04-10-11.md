By the end of the second week of Database Systems, I already had a rough sense of what a database was trying to do. What I did not yet have was a formal way to express that sense — a language precise enough that another person could read my design and understand exactly what I had intended. That language arrived this week, in the form of entity-relationship diagrams, and learning it turned out to be harder and more interesting than I had expected.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad introduced ER modeling as the step that comes before any SQL, before any normalization, before any table is created. Before you can build a database, you need a clear picture of the world you are trying to represent: what kinds of things exist in that world, what properties each kind of thing has, and how the different kinds of things relate to each other. In the formal language of ER modeling, these are entities, attributes, and relationships. An entity is any distinct thing worth tracking — a patient, a doctor, a hospital ward. An attribute is a property of that entity worth recording — a patient's age, their blood pressure, their diagnosis. A relationship captures how entities connect — a patient is treated by a doctor, a doctor works in a ward.

The vocabulary itself was not the challenge. The challenge was applying it to scenarios that were not perfectly neat, where the right answer was not obvious and different reasonable choices led to different tradeoffs. Dr. Ahmad gave us a series of case studies — a clinic appointment system, a small retail inventory, a university course registration system — and asked us to produce ER diagrams for each one. Every single case contained at least one decision point I had not anticipated. Should an address be a single attribute or broken into separate fields for street, city, and postal code? Should a doctor and a patient be modeled as two separate entities, or as one entity with a role attribute that distinguishes them? Is the relationship between a student and a course one-to-many or many-to-many?

That last question was the one that caught me most off guard. My instinct said one-to-many — a student takes one course at a time. The reality, which a quick correction from Dr. Ahmad made immediately obvious, is that a student takes many courses simultaneously, and each course has many students enrolled in it. The relationship is many-to-many, and it requires its own intermediate structure to represent correctly in a relational schema. My first instinct had been shaped by what was simplest to model, rather than by what was actually true about the world. That is exactly the kind of mistake that normalization is designed to catch later — but catching it at the ER stage, before any tables are built, saves an enormous amount of rework.

Drawing these diagrams by hand, repeatedly, across multiple different scenarios, built a kind of fluency that reading about ER modeling could not have produced on its own. There is something about the physical act of drawing a box, deciding what to put inside it, drawing a line to another box, and choosing the notation for the cardinality of that relationship that forces a specificity you can avoid in a verbal description. By the middle of this week I was sketching rough ER diagrams almost automatically whenever anyone described any system to me — a habit that had clearly become part of how I process the structure of any real-world problem, not just problems labeled as database exercises.

Looking ahead from this point in the semester, I can say now that this week built the foundation for everything the heart disease prediction project would later demand. When our group began designing the schema for that project months later, we went straight to ER thinking — identifying the patient as the central entity, their clinical measurements as attributes, the relationships between patients and their recorded visits as the first structural decision we needed to resolve. The fact that we could do this quickly and cleanly was a direct result of the hours spent in this week drawing and revising and arguing over diagrams in the lab.

The revision process itself was something Dr. Ahmad made feel normal rather than embarrassing. When he looked at our first attempts in class, he never treated a flawed diagram as a failure. He treated it as a draft, and the conversation he opened about what was missing or imprecise was more educational than any version I could have produced by getting it right the first time. That attitude — that a wrong first attempt is the expected and useful starting point for any real design problem — is one I tried to carry into every technical decision I made for the rest of the semester.

I also found, during this week, that the habit of reading problem statements more slowly that PF had started to build in me was applying directly here as well. Every noun in a description of a real-world system is a potential entity. Every verb is a potential relationship. Every adjective attached to a noun is a potential attribute. Reading that way, underlining as I went, meant I arrived at the diagram phase with a far more complete picture of what needed to be represented than I had when I used to read a specification once and immediately start drawing. That slower reading habit, and what it produced in the quality of my ER designs, is one of the things I now associate most directly with what [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) was trying to build in us across both courses this semester.
