Two days after my first real struggle in Programming Fundamentals, I found myself in a different classroom for the opening lecture of Database Systems. The subject was new, the room was different, but the professor was the same — Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who teaches us both courses this semester. What struck me immediately was how different the opening felt from PF. In PF, the first question had been: can you break a process down into precise enough steps for a machine to follow? In Database Systems, the first question was something entirely different: can you represent the real world accurately enough that a structure built around it actually holds up?

Dr. Bilal Ahmad opened with an idea that sounded simple but turned out to be one of the most important things I heard all semester: a database is not just storage. It is a model of some part of the real world, built deliberately enough that the relationships and rules of that real world are preserved inside the data structure itself. A spreadsheet can hold information, but a spreadsheet does not know that a student belongs to a department, or that a department can have many students, or that removing a department should not silently erase every student in it. A properly designed database knows all of these things, because the structure itself encodes them as rules and constraints, not just as values sitting in cells.

The example that made this stick for me was a comparison to a library. An unorganized room full of books is technically a collection of information, but finding anything in it is slow and unreliable. A library catalog — organized by author, title, subject, cross-referenced and indexed — is a database. It was designed in advance so that any search through it is fast and predictable. Once Dr. Ahmad put it that way, I started noticing database thinking everywhere — in how a hospital tracks patient records, in how a university manages enrollment, in how a pharmacy keeps prescriptions organized so that any pharmacist on any shift can find what they need immediately. The idea of structured information as a design problem, rather than just a storage problem, was genuinely new to me.

The first few lectures worked through what Dr. Ahmad called the relational model — the foundational idea that data lives in tables, each table represents one kind of thing, and the connections between different kinds of things are captured through shared keys rather than by duplicating information. This was different from anything I had been thinking about in PF. In PF, data lives inside a running program, held in variables and arrays that disappear when the program ends. Here, the data is meant to outlast any single program, to be accessed by many different users simultaneously, and to stay consistent even as it grows to a scale that no in-memory data structure could handle.

What I found most effective about these early lectures was the method Dr. Ahmad used to introduce each new idea. He never started with a definition. He always started with a real scenario — a small clinic, a simple online store, a university registration system — and asked the class to try to identify what needed to be stored and how it should be organized before he introduced any formal vocabulary. Only after we had struggled to describe the structure in ordinary language would he show us how the formal concepts — entities, attributes, relationships, primary keys — gave us a precise way to say what we had been trying to say in plain words. That sequence, intuition before formalism, is one I found far more effective than the reverse.

Even in these very first lectures, Dr. Ahmad planted a seed about where the course was going. He mentioned that our semester project would involve not just database design but also a machine learning component, and that he always pushes his students toward datasets that come from domains where the data is collected with genuine rigor. Medical data, he explained, is his preferred choice precisely because clinical measurements are taken by trained professionals under controlled conditions, which means the data itself is of a quality that supports reliable model training. At the time, this felt like a footnote to an introductory lecture. Looking back, it was a signal about everything the second half of the semester was going to demand.

By the end of that first week, I had several pages of rough notes that already looked like early ER diagrams, even though I did not yet know that term. Just the instinct — draw a box for each kind of thing, draw a line between things that are connected — was already forming. Those early sketches bore almost no resemblance to the polished schema our group would eventually produce for the heart disease prediction project that became the centerpiece of the semester. But the impulse behind them was the same, and I think that impulse was exactly what the first week was trying to plant.

Sitting with both courses now in their opening weeks, the contrast was already becoming interesting. PF was asking me to think about process — what happens first, then second, then third. Database Systems was asking me to think about structure — what exists, what properties it has, how it connects to other things that exist. Both are forms of precision. Both punish vagueness. But they demand precision about different things. It would take months of working through both simultaneously before I fully understood that [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) was teaching two versions of the same underlying discipline — the discipline of turning something messy into something a machine can work with reliably.
