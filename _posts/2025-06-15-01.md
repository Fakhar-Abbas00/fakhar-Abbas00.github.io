A few weeks into building this portfolio blog, I found myself stepping back and asking a question I had not really considered before I started writing: is this actually a good practice for a student to take on, documenting an entire academic journey in public, semester by semester? Building a portfolio website like this was something I was encouraged to try as a Computer Engineering student, with the idea being that it leaves a kind of digital footprint, a record of how I was thinking and growing at this particular stage of my education. The more I have written, the more I have come to believe it genuinely is a useful practice, not because every post turns out polished, but because the act of writing things down forces a kind of honesty and reflection that simply thinking about an experience never quite manages to produce on its own.

There are a few concrete benefits I have noticed so far. The first is that it gives shape to experiences that would otherwise blur together in memory. A semester full of classes, deadlines, and small daily struggles tends to compress into a vague impression of "it was hard" if you never sit down and actually describe the individual pieces of it. Writing forces specificity. The second benefit is that it creates a record I can actually return to later, not just for nostalgia, but as a way of tracking how my thinking and confidence have shifted over time, something that is nearly impossible to judge accurately from memory alone. And the third, more practical benefit, is that it builds a body of writing and reflection that exists independently of any single transcript or exam result, which feels like a more honest representation of what a degree actually involves.

With that said, I want to use this post to go back to where this whole journey actually started, since the reflection above only makes sense once the story behind it is on the page.

Assalam o Alaikum everyone. I wanted to start this blog by sharing something a little more personal than a typical project write-up: the story of my first two semesters as a Computer Engineering student, especially everything I went through in Programming Fundamentals and Database Management Systems. I am writing this not because it was a perfect journey, but because it absolutely was not, and I think that is exactly why it is worth writing down.

When I look back at the past year, academics were only one part of the picture. There was also the personal growth that came with leaving behind a familiar routine and stepping into something I did not fully understand yet. There was the discomfort of learning new skills from zero, the frustration of hitting walls I could not immediately climb over, and the slow, sometimes uneven process of adapting to a university environment that was nothing like the one I had imagined for myself in school. None of that shows up neatly in a transcript, but it shaped me just as much as any exam did.

I think a lot of students who are new to computer science assume that everyone around them already has some kind of head start, and in my case, that assumption was not entirely wrong. Many of my classmates had touched a keyboard with real intent long before I had. I had not. That gap was real, and pretending it was not there would have only made things harder for me later. So this series of posts is partly a record of how I tried to close that gap, semester by semester, subject by subject.

To explain exactly where that gap came from, I should go back further than university itself. I completed my Matric with a Biology group, and after that I studied Pre-Engineering for my FSc. Looking at that academic path on paper, you would not immediately guess that I would end up in Computer Engineering. Biology and Pre-Engineering gave me a strong foundation in subjects like physics, chemistry, and mathematics, but they gave me almost no exposure to computers, programming, or anything resembling software development.

This is the part of my story that I think is worth being upfront about. Many of my classmates in Computer Engineering came in with at least some prior coding experience, whether from school clubs, personal projects, or simply growing up more immersed in technology. I did not have that. Before joining university, my interaction with computers was mostly limited to basic, everyday use. I had never written a line of code. I did not know what a compiler was. Concepts like algorithms or data structures were not even on my radar.

So when I actually entered the field of Computer Engineering, it felt like stepping into a room where everyone else already knew the local language and I was still learning the alphabet. That is not an exaggeration of how it felt in those first few weeks. It was both exciting, because everything was new and full of possibility, and genuinely intimidating, because I could sense the gap between where I was and where I needed to be.

I remember being very aware, almost from day one, that I would have to put in more effort than students who already had some programming background. This was not a complaint, just an honest assessment of my starting point. I knew that if I wanted to keep pace with my class, I could not rely on natural familiarity the way some of my peers could. I would have to build that familiarity from scratch, through deliberate practice and a willingness to ask questions even when it felt uncomfortable to admit I did not understand something basic.

Looking back now, I think this background actually shaped my work ethic in a useful way. Because I knew I was starting behind, I did not take anything for granted. I could not afford to skim through lecture material and assume I would pick it up later, because for me, picking things up later usually meant falling further behind. I had to engage with the material actively from the very beginning, which, while exhausting at times, also meant that whatever I did learn, I learned thoroughly.

There is also something to be said about the mental shift required to move from a Biology and Pre-Engineering mindset into a computing one. In my earlier studies, a lot of the learning was about memorization, understanding biological systems, or solving physics and math problems with fairly established methods. Programming, as I would soon discover, demanded a different kind of thinking entirely, one built around logic, abstraction, and problem-solving from first principles. That transition alone took real adjustment, separate from just learning new syntax or tools.

What I want this blog to be, more than anything, is honest. I do not want to write polished success stories where everything worked out the way I had planned, because that is not what happened. There were setbacks, disappointing midterms, long days as a commuting student, and a lot of moments where I doubted whether this field was really for me. But there were also small wins that meant a lot at the time: finally understanding a loop that had confused me for a week, finishing a project I thought was beyond me, and slowly realizing that I was capable of more than I gave myself credit for at the start.

If you are reading this and you are in a similar position, coming into Computer Engineering or any computing-related field without a strong technical background, I want you to know that it is a real challenge, but not an impossible one. It just means your first semester might look a little different from someone else's, and that is completely fine.

In the next post, I will talk about how I actually ended up choosing Computer Engineering in the first place, since it was not my first choice of field, and the decision took some real reflection before I came around to it, and about my first day at university, where that decision stopped being abstract and started becoming my everyday reality.
