By the first week of June, I could draw an ER diagram for most small scenarios without too much difficulty, and I had a working understanding of what a relational database was trying to achieve. What I did not yet understand was why a table design that looked completely reasonable on the surface could still be hiding serious problems that would only become visible once real data started flowing through it. The normalization unit was designed to close exactly that gap, and it turned out to be one of the most demanding and most rewarding weeks of the entire Database Systems course.

Normalization is a set of rules for organizing data into tables in a way that removes redundancy and prevents specific kinds of errors. Before this week, my approach to table design was instinctive and untested: if a piece of information seemed related to the thing I was modeling, I put it in the same table. The result was tables that held far more than they should, with information repeated across many rows in ways that made the data fragile. Dr. Bilal Ahmad spent the first lecture of this unit demonstrating, with a carefully chosen example, exactly how fragile that kind of design really is.

The example involved a table where a department name was stored on every single row belonging to a student in that department. If the department changed its name, every row would need to be updated — and if even one was missed, the table would now contain two different names for the same department, making it impossible to know which one was correct. Worse, if a department had no students yet, it could not even be recorded in the table, since the department's information had nowhere to live without a student attached to it. These were not artificial problems invented to make the concept seem dramatic. They were real anomalies that arise in production systems built without normalization, and they cause real damage to data integrity over time.

We worked through the normal forms in sequence. First Normal Form established the baseline: every column holds a single, atomic value. No comma-separated lists hiding in a field, no multiple phone numbers crammed into one column. Identifying 1NF violations turned out to be the most straightforward part of the unit, because they tend to be visible the moment you know to look for them. Second Normal Form was harder. It required understanding partial dependencies — situations where a non-key attribute depends on only part of a composite primary key rather than the whole thing. I spent a full lab session working through examples before the distinction between a partial dependency and a full dependency became reliable rather than guesswork.

Third Normal Form was the most conceptually subtle. A transitive dependency exists when a non-key attribute depends on another non-key attribute rather than depending directly on the primary key. The example that finally made this clear involved a table where a student record included both the student's advisor and that advisor's office number. The office number does not really belong to the student — it belongs to the advisor. Storing it in the student table creates a transitive dependency that will cause problems if the advisor ever moves offices, because every student record connected to that advisor would need to be updated rather than just the advisor's own record. Recognizing this kind of indirect dependency required a different quality of attention than the earlier normal forms had demanded — a willingness to follow the chain of dependency one step further than the obvious first check.

What made this material genuinely settle in was a technique Dr. Ahmad encouraged throughout the unit: start with a messy, unnormalized table, identify all the anomalies it would cause in practice, and then work through the normal forms as the cure for each specific problem. This backward approach — problem first, solution second — made each normal form feel like an answer to something concrete rather than an arbitrary rule to memorize. By the end of the unit I could look at an unfamiliar table and predict, before any formal analysis, roughly what kind of data integrity problems it was likely to create downstream.

The normalization unit also laid the groundwork for something that would matter enormously a few weeks later. Dr. Ahmad mentioned at the close of the unit that our project would require us to apply these principles to a real medical dataset, and that getting the normalization right before building anything else was not optional — it was the foundation everything else would stand on. When our group eventually sat down to design the schema for the heart disease prediction system, I found myself going back to this unit's checklist almost automatically: atomic values, full key dependency, no transitive dependencies lurking between non-key attributes. The care we put into the schema at that stage showed up later in the reliability of the data our model was trained on, and I do not think the connection is coincidental.
There was something that surprised me about this unit that went beyond the technical content: the practice of normalization changed how I read problem descriptions generally. Before this week, I would read a project description and think immediately about what to build. After this week, I found myself reading problem descriptions and instinctively asking: what are the distinct things being described here, and are any of them being conflated in ways that will cause problems later? That habit — looking for hidden dependencies and conflated concepts before committing to a structure — is not unique to database design. It turns out to apply just as directly to the heart disease prediction project we would build later, where distinguishing clearly between patient-level data and visit-level data early in the design process saved our group from a significant rework that a less careful first pass would have required.


It is also, in hindsight, one of the clearest examples of how [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) structures a course — each unit building quietly toward the next, in ways that only become fully visible when you look back from the end.
