I will be honest: I walked into my first Programming Fundamentals lecture thinking I had a head start. I had tinkered with code before, done reasonably well in school mathematics, and convinced myself that the leap into a proper Computer Engineering course would be manageable. That confidence lasted almost exactly two weeks before the course stripped it away and replaced it with something far more useful — a genuine understanding of what it actually means to think precisely enough for a machine to follow you.

Programming Fundamentals, which we all quickly started calling PF, is one of the foundation stones of Computer Engineering at UET Lahore, Faisalabad Campus. I am in Section A, and from the very first lab session it was clear that this course was not going to be gentle with overconfidence. The task sounds simple when you describe it to someone outside the field: take a problem that you could explain in a sentence and turn it into instructions so exact, so unambiguous, that a machine with absolutely no common sense can execute them perfectly every single time. In practice, this is one of the hardest mental habits to build, and the early weeks of PF were where I first began to understand how far I was from having it.

The opening weeks covered variables, data types, input and output, and the basic structure of a program. None of these topics sounded threatening on paper. The first real test came when our professor asked us to write a program that accepted three numbers and printed them in ascending order — without using any sorting function. I expected to finish in a few minutes. I spent close to forty on it instead, because I kept writing solutions that worked for some combinations of numbers and silently produced wrong answers for others. That experience was my introduction to what correctness actually means in programming: not that it worked once, but that it works every single time, including the cases you did not think to test.

What the early weeks of PF were really asking of me was a complete change in how I approached problems before writing a single line of code. In everyday life, we skip steps constantly. We rely on context. We let ambiguity resolve itself through shared understanding. Programming refuses all of that. There is no shared understanding between you and a compiler. There is only what you wrote, interpreted exactly as written, with no allowance for what you meant. Learning to accept that, rather than fight it, was the real work of these first weeks, and it did not happen in one afternoon. It happened slowly, through a series of small humiliations spread out over several lab sessions.

One habit that formed during this period has stayed with me all semester: writing out the steps of a solution by hand, on paper, before opening a code editor. It felt slow at first, almost like cheating myself out of time I could be spending coding. But every time I skipped this step, I paid for it later in bugs that took far longer to track down than the planning step would have taken. The paper-first approach forced me to commit to a sequence of steps before worrying about syntax, which meant that by the time I opened the editor, I was translating a plan rather than inventing one on the fly. The difference in the quality of the code I produced was immediate and obvious.

I also started to notice something during these early weeks that I had never thought about before: the names you give things in a program are a form of communication. A variable called `x` and a variable called `student_average` do exactly the same computational job, but one of them tells a story to anyone who reads the code later — including yourself, a week after you wrote it, when you have forgotten your own reasoning. Good naming, I came to understand, is not cosmetic. It is part of what makes code possible to maintain, to debug, and to hand to another person without requiring a long explanation. Our lab sessions made this concrete in a way that no lecture alone could have, because it was only when I came back to my own messy, hastily named code and genuinely could not follow it that the lesson stuck.

The lab environment itself was part of the education. Section A's lab sessions had a particular energy — a mix of concentration and low-level panic that, in hindsight, was exactly the right atmosphere for learning. When a program refuses to compile and you cannot immediately see why, the instinct is to randomly change things and hope the problem disappears. I did this more than once in the early weeks. It never worked reliably, and the habit I eventually replaced it with — reading the error message carefully, tracing back through the code line by line, and forming a hypothesis before making any change — is one of the most valuable things the course gave me.

By the end of these opening weeks, the outlines of a new way of thinking were beginning to form. I did not have a name for it yet. I just knew that something was shifting in how I approached problems generally, not just programming problems. A couple of weeks later, when I sat down in a very different lecture hall for the first session of Database Systems — also taught by [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) — I would begin to understand that the discipline I was building in PF was exactly the foundation that second course was going to build upon.
