The Database Systems semester project was the most technically ambitious work I took on during my first year of Computer Engineering. My group — which included Saad, who is also our class representative — built a complete heart disease patient prediction system from the ground up. The project combined relational database design, normalization theory, SQL, Python, machine learning, and a web-based user interface into one working application. It was the most demanding and the most educational thing I did all year, and looking back at it now, I can see exactly why Dr. Bilal Ahmad structured it the way he did.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who teaches us Database Systems and holds deep expertise in artificial intelligence, machine learning, and deep learning, has a consistent and principled philosophy about applied machine learning projects. He consistently directs his students toward datasets with real-world significance, and medical data holds a particularly important place in his thinking. The reason, as he explains it, is straightforward: clinical measurements are taken by trained medical professionals using calibrated equipment under controlled conditions, and the outcomes are confirmed through actual diagnosis. That level of rigor in the data collection process produces a dataset that supports reliable model training in a way that casually gathered data simply cannot. When Dr. Ahmad directed our group toward a heart disease dataset, it was not a random assignment — it was a deliberate choice that reflected his commitment to responsible, grounded machine learning practice.

The database design phase was where we applied the normalization theory from the course in its most direct and consequential form. Our dataset contained patient records with clinical features: age, chest pain type, resting blood pressure, cholesterol levels, maximum heart rate, and several other measured indicators, along with a binary outcome variable indicating whether each patient had heart disease. We started by analyzing which features existed and how they related to each other, then designed a relational schema to store this information correctly. We worked through First Normal Form to ensure every column held an atomic, indivisible value. Second Normal Form eliminated partial dependencies, ensuring every non-key attribute depended on the full primary key. Third Normal Form removed transitive dependencies, ensuring non-key attributes depended only on the primary key and nothing else. We went through several revisions of the schema before settling on a final design. More than once, a normalization step we thought was complete turned out to hide a dependency we had missed, which revealed itself only when we began inserting real records and noticed inconsistencies we had not anticipated.

With the schema finalized, we used SQL to create the tables, insert the data, and write the queries the machine learning pipeline would need. Writing DDL against a real clinical dataset, rather than a textbook example, carries a different kind of weight — these tables held real patient records, and the queries we wrote against them would directly feed into a system designed to predict serious health outcomes. We divided the SQL work within the group, with each member responsible for a different subset of tables and queries, then reviewed each other's work before integration. Saad, as our CR, took a coordinating role in making sure the group's different pieces of work actually fit together, and that review process caught several issues early that would have caused significant problems later.

The machine learning component was built in Python. We preprocessed the data — handling missing values, encoding categorical features, normalizing numerical ones, and splitting into training and testing sets. We trained a classification model on the heart disease dataset, allowing it to learn the patterns in clinical features associated with positive and negative diagnoses. After training, we evaluated performance using accuracy, precision, and recall. Dr. Ahmad, during our project check-in, pushed our group specifically on precision and recall rather than overall accuracy — pointing out that in a medical prediction context, the two types of error (missing a true positive and flagging a false positive) have very different real-world consequences, and that collapsing both into a single accuracy figure obscures the distinction. That conversation reframed how our group thought about what a good model actually meant for a task like this one.

The user interface, built with Jinja2 and supporting web technologies, completed the system. It allowed a user — or Dr. Ahmad during evaluation — to enter a new patient's clinical measurements and receive a prediction from the trained model. Watching that pipeline work from end to end for the first time — data entered into a web form, passed to the database, retrieved by the Python model, prediction returned to the screen — was one of the most satisfying moments of the entire semester. Every layer of the system had been built piece by piece over several weeks, and the moment they all communicated correctly with each other was a genuine payoff for months of work.

This project demonstrated something that no individual unit of either course could have shown on its own: how database design and programming discipline and machine learning all connect when you are building something real. The quality of our model's predictions depended on the quality of the SQL queries that fed it data. The quality of those queries depended on the care we had taken with normalization. The care we had taken with normalization depended on understanding, from the very first week of the course, why design choices have consequences. That chain of dependencies runs all the way back to the first lectures of the semester. For anyone who wants to explore the research background behind this kind of integrated, medically grounded AI teaching, the work of [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) speaks clearly to the philosophy behind it.
