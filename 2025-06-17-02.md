Writing the previous posts in this series made me think more carefully about why I am even doing this, putting deeply personal decisions and uncertain first impressions out in public for anyone to read. Is documenting something this personal, a career decision I did not fully believe in at first and the disorientation of a literal first day, actually a useful practice, or does it just expose more than it is worth? Having sat with that question while writing this post, I think the honesty is exactly the point. A polished version of this story, where I confidently chose Computer Engineering and walked into university feeling sure of myself, would not be true, and it would not be useful to anyone reading it either. The value of writing this down comes specifically from showing the uncertainty, not editing it out.

There is a real benefit, I think, in seeing how a decision that initially feels like a compromise can still turn into something genuine later, and in seeing that the disorientation of a first day is not a personal failing but something nearly everyone goes through quietly at the same time. Reading something like that, rather than just being told "things will work out," is more convincing precisely because it is specific and personal rather than generic reassurance. With that reasoning laid out, I want to go back and actually tell both of these stories properly.

People often assume that students end up in a particular field because it was always their dream, but my path into Computer Engineering did not start that way at all. If I am being completely honest, my first preference was actually Law. I had also seriously considered Civil Engineering as an alternative. Computer Engineering was not on my radar in any meaningful way when I was finishing my FSc and thinking about what came next.

The decision to switch direction came after a series of conversations with my family about future opportunities. We talked through different fields, weighed the practical realities of each, and eventually I was encouraged to consider Computer Engineering instead of Law. I will not pretend this was an easy adjustment to make mentally. Law had been something I pictured for myself for a while, and stepping away from that vision was not simple, even if the reasoning behind the decision made sense on paper.

In the beginning, accepting this change felt more like a compromise than a choice I had actually made for myself. I remember feeling a bit disconnected from the decision, like I was moving into a field that other people thought was right for me rather than one I had chosen with full conviction. That is an uncomfortable place to start a degree from, and I think it is worth being honest about that instead of pretending I walked into Computer Engineering with total enthusiasm from day one.

What changed things for me was simply giving the field a real chance once I was actually in it. As I started exploring what Computer Engineering actually involved, beyond the assumptions I had walked in with, I began to see it differently. I started to notice just how much room there is in this field for growth, innovation, and impact. Technology touches nearly every other industry at this point, and the more I learned about the breadth of what Computer Engineering could lead to, the more I started to genuinely engage with it rather than just go through the motions.

This shift did not happen overnight. It was a gradual realization, built up over weeks of classes, conversations, and small moments of curiosity that started to outweigh my initial reluctance. Looking back now, after going through Programming Fundamentals and Database Management Systems, I do not regret the path I ended up on, even though it took me a while to arrive at that feeling honestly rather than just accepting it because the decision had already been made. There is a real difference between tolerating a decision and actually believing in it, and for me, that belief came later, through experience, not through the initial conversation that set this path in motion.

That belief was still nowhere in sight, though, on the actual first day I walked onto campus. There is a particular kind of nervous energy that comes with a true first day, the kind where you are not sure exactly what to expect but you know that whatever happens is going to set the tone for a long time afterward. That is exactly how my first day at university felt. I walked in carrying a strange mix of excitement and anxiety, and looking back, I think most of my classmates were feeling some version of the same thing, even if nobody said it out loud.

The day began with orientation, and this is where things started to feel real for the first time. Our Head of Department stood in front of all of us and walked us through the university environment, the academic rules we would be expected to follow, and what would be expected of us over the coming years. I remember sitting there trying to absorb everything, even though a lot of it felt abstract at the time, since I had no real frame of reference yet for what daily university life would actually look like.

Everything about that day felt unfamiliar in a way that is hard to fully describe unless you have lived through it yourself. The campus itself was new territory. The faces around me were new. Even simple things, like figuring out where a particular classroom was or how the timing between classes worked, required a level of attention that would later become second nature but felt overwhelming in the moment.

Meeting new classmates was probably the part of the day that stuck with me the most. There is something both comforting and slightly nerve-wracking about realizing that everyone around you is in the same position, starting fresh, not quite sure who their friends are going to be yet, but hoping to find some sense of community fairly soon. Some of those early conversations turned into friendships that carried me through the harder parts of the semester later on, which is something I did not anticipate on day one but came to genuinely appreciate.

Listening to experienced faculty members speak during orientation also left an impression on me. There was a noticeable difference between hearing about university life in the abstract, from family or from general expectations, and actually hearing it directly from people who had been teaching and working within that environment for years. It made the experience feel more grounded, and it gave me a clearer sense that I was not just attending another level of schooling, but stepping into something structurally different from anything I had experienced before.

By the end of that first day, I remember feeling a quiet but distinct realization settle in: a new chapter of my life had genuinely begun, and there was no going back to the version of student life I was used to before. That realization carried a mix of emotions, some excitement about what was ahead, but also a real awareness that I was going to need to adapt quickly, especially given how unfamiliar I was with the technical side of what I had signed up for.
